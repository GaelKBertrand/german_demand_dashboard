What this tab answers: For each of the five role groups tracked in this dashboard, this tab maps (1) the Indian qualification a candidate must hold — including its NSQF and NHEQF level — (2) which Indian framework governs their training standards, and (3) what the German qualification recognition (Berufsanerkennung) process looks like in practice: who the competent authority is, what typical outcomes are, and what compensatory measures are required before a candidate can practise independently. The goal is to give GATI a precise, sourced reference for programme design, candidate briefing, and employer expectation-setting.
Scope and methodology: Covers ISCO groups 222, 325, 226, 532, and 221. Indian framework data: NSQF levels from NCVET / NQR (nqr.gov.in); NHEQF levels from UGC (applies to university-awarded degrees only — INC diplomas, GNM, and ANM fall outside NHEQF scope); QP-NOS codes from HSSC (applicable to vocational/NSDC-certified roles only; INC- and council-regulated degrees use separate curriculum standards). German recognition data: ANABIN / KMK (anabin.kmk.org), BQ-Portal (bq-portal.de), and the official recognition portal (anerkennung-in-deutschland.de). Recognition outcomes are indicative — actual decisions vary by German state and the specific institution a qualification comes from. Always verify individual cases before candidate briefing.

What to expect in Germany: Indian nursing qualifications (B.Sc and GNM) almost universally receive partial recognition (teilweise Gleichwertigkeit) in Germany — around 45% of all nursing recognition procedures in 2024 required compensatory measures. Candidates must complete an Anpassungslehrgang (supervised practice period) before obtaining the protected title of Pflegefachfrau/Pflegefachmann. Duration varies by state and individual assessment: typically 6–12 months for B.Sc Nursing graduates, and up to 24–36 months for GNM holders where there are greater competency gaps. A Berufserlaubnis (temporary practice permit) allows paid supervised work during this period. German B2 is required to start; the Fachsprachenprüfung (FSP) — a medical German oral examination — must be passed before full independent registration.

🇩🇪 German Qualification Recognition — Berufsanerkennung
Governing law: Pflegeberufegesetz (PflBG), enacted 2017, in force Jan 2020  |  ANABIN (anabin.kmk.org)  |  BQ-Portal (bq-portal.de)
Competent authority: State health ministries (Landesbehörden) — varies by German state. The authority checks whether the Indian nursing curriculum meets German standards for hours, subject coverage, and clinical training. Most Indian qualifications show gaps in areas such as geriatric care, palliative care, and psychiatric nursing content, leading to partial recognition.
QualificationTypical OutcomeCompensatory MeasureApprox. Duration
B.Sc Nursing (INC-recognised) Partial equivalence Anpassungslehrgang (supervised practice at accredited hospital) 6–12 months (state-dependent)
GNM (INC-recognised) Partial equivalence Anpassungslehrgang or Kenntnisprüfung (knowledge exam) 12–36 months
ANM No direct pathway Complete Pflegehelferin-Ausbildung (1 year) or full Pflegefachfrau (3 years) 1–3 years training from scratch
Language requirements: B2 German is required before starting the Anpassungslehrgang. The Fachsprachenprüfung (FSP) — a structured medical German oral exam testing doctor-patient communication, ward handover, and documentation — must be passed before full registration as Pflegefachfrau/mann. A Berufserlaubnis (temporary work permit under §10 PflBG, valid up to 2 years) allows supervised paid work while the adaptation period is underway. Free advisory services: NETZWERK IQ, anerkennung-in-deutschland.de.
Critical structural constraint for GATI: The two most in-demand ISCO 325 roles in Germany — Medizinische Fachangestellte (MFA) and Zahnmedizinische Fachangestellte (ZFA) — are German dual-system apprenticeship roles with no Indian equivalent. They are not professions that exist in India's healthcare system; they combine clinical tasks with administrative and billing functions specific to the German healthcare context. Indian candidates cannot enter MFA or ZFA roles through a recognition process — they must complete the full 3-year German vocational training (Ausbildung) under BBiG (Berufsbildungsgesetz). By contrast, Indian Physiotherapy (DPT/BPT) and Occupational Therapy (DOT/BOT) graduates can seek recognition under the relevant German professional law — but BPT (degree level) has a much stronger case than DPT (diploma level).

What to expect in Germany: Degree-level allied health professions (BPT, BDS, B.Pharm, B.Sc Audiology/SLP) each follow a separate German professional law. All typically result in partial equivalence with a required Ausgleichsmaßnahme (either an adaptation course or knowledge exam). BPT graduates face a training-hours comparison: Germany requires 4,500 hours (2,900 theory + 1,600 clinical) — Indian BPT programmes typically cover 4.5 years with substantial clinical time, but exact curriculum-to-curriculum comparison is done by the competent state authority. Dentists and pharmacists must pass the Fachsprachenprüfung (FSP) before full Approbation/licence. Physiotherapists and speech-language pathologists do not require the FSP — B2/C1 general German is sufficient.

Note: No Berufsanerkennung process exists for GDA-level qualifications. The pathways below represent alternative in-Germany training routes.
Germany's care workforce is divided into three tiers: (1) Pflegefachfrau/mann (3-year Ausbildung, full care professional — regulated under PflBG); (2) Pflegehilfskraft/Pflegehelferin (1-year state-regulated helper course); and (3) unqualified care support workers. Indian GDA holders most naturally map to the Pflegehilfskraft tier, but this requires completing German training — not recognition of the Indian credential.

Care sector employers in Germany often sponsor Ausbildung for motivated international candidates — this is a viable but longer-term placement route for GDA-trained individuals.
What to expect in Germany: German medical licensing (Approbation) for non-EU doctors is governed by the Bundesärzteordnung (BÄO). Indian MBBS graduates typically cannot obtain direct Approbation — the process involves: (1) submitting an equivalence assessment (Gutachten) of the Indian curriculum; (2) passing the Fachsprachenprüfung (FSP) — a medical German oral exam assessing doctor-patient communication, ward handover, and documentation; and (3) if equivalence is not established, sitting a Kenntnisprüfung (oral clinical knowledge exam). A Berufserlaubnis (§10 BÄO, temporary 2-year permit) allows supervised paid work during the process. Language requirement is C1 German for independent medical practice — higher than for nursing or physiotherapy.
